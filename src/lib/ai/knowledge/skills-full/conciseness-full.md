# Skill: Conciseness

> "Maximum signal per word. Tight sentences over bloated ones."

Conciseness is the discipline of saying the most with the fewest words that will still land. It's distinct from clarity (which is about being understood on the first hearing) — a sentence can be perfectly clear AND bloated. Conciseness asks: did every word earn its place?

## Definition

Signal-per-word density, plus time-budget discipline. A concise speaker cuts filler, avoids repetition, skips preambles, and knows when to stop. Conciseness shows up in two places: the word level (no throat-clearing, no hedges) and the structural level (one main point, not three; one supporting example, not five).

## Why we chose this

Conciseness is where credibility either holds or leaks. We chose it because filler density and hedge rate are the two highest-signal failure modes a listener registers — and they're directly measurable from a transcript with no model needed. Trainable because the rep itself is the training: 60 seconds of no-um, no-hedge speaking is a cognitive exercise that rewires the reflex. The fundamental reason: every word that doesn't move the argument actively undoes the argument.

## Experts and sources

- **Strunk & White**, *The Elements of Style* — "Omit needless words" is the textbook conciseness rule; conciseness is arguably the most durable thing the book teaches.
- **William Zinsser**, *On Writing Well* — "Every word that serves no function... is an enemy of the sentence."
- **Paul Graham** — essay prose as a model of conciseness: one idea per sentence, one argument per paragraph.
- **Barbara Minto**, *The Pyramid Principle* — BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front) is conciseness at the structural level.
- **Patrick Winston** (MIT), "How to Speak" — the "promise, inspiration, enumeration" opening is about getting to signal fast.
- **30 Minutes to President's Club** (2024) — modern cold-call research showing concise pitches convert dramatically better.

## What great conciseness sounds like

- Main point stated first, no preamble (BLUF pattern)
- Each sentence advances the argument — no sentence is load-bearing only for tone
- Filler rate < 2 per minute (um, uh, like, you know, basically)
- Finishes within 10% of time budget — neither rushes the last point nor strands time at the end
- Tight word economy — typically < 25 words per distinct point
- No repetition — the same idea isn't restated in different words because the first statement was weak

## What low conciseness sounds like

- High filler rate (> 4 per minute)
- Long preambles — "so what I want to say is, basically, the thing I think is important here is…"
- Repeating the same point in slightly different words because the first statement didn't feel strong
- Going over time budget — the speaker keeps talking past where the content runs out
- Hedge-stacking — "I think it's probably the case that maybe we could consider perhaps…"

## Signals (deterministic contribution)

- **Filler rate** — strong signal; conciseness and delivery both reference this metric
- **Time-budget ratio** — going over budget is a conciseness failure even when the content is fine
- **Words-per-point** (derived from transcript segmentation) — high values = bloat
- **Repetition detection** — same clause/idea stated twice in one rep

## Signals (LLM contribution)

- Content-density judgment — did each sentence add signal, or was it padding?
- Preamble detection — did the rep lead with the main point or warm into it?
- Hedge-stacking pattern — is every assertion weakened by qualifiers?

## How to improve conciseness

- **BLUF your openings.** State the answer before the rationale.
- **Cut one sentence per rewrite.** On every practice rep, challenge yourself to drop a sentence and see if the rep still lands.
- **Time constraints force the skill.** Cognify's `be_concise` rep type drills this directly with 20-second time boxes.
- **Silence after the point.** Conciseness includes knowing when to stop. Ending clean is a skill.
- **Listen for your own hedges.** Record a normal conversation; count every "I think", "maybe", "kind of". The number surprises most speakers.

## Common conciseness traps

- **Filler as a stalling tactic** — filler isn't accidental; it's a cognitive-load release. Training thinking quality (coherent generation) reduces filler indirectly.
- **Verbosity as safety** — speakers pad to feel thorough. Thoroughness and conciseness aren't opposites; a concise point feels MORE authoritative, not less.
- **Restating as emphasis** — repeating a claim to underline it often dilutes instead. Better: one clean statement + a concrete example.
- **Filling time** — if you have 60 seconds and your answer is 40, stop at 40. Talking to fill time is the cheapest conciseness failure.
