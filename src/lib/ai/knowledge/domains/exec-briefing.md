# Domain: Executive Briefings & Presentations

Communicating with senior leaders — C-suite, VPs, board members — under time pressure and with high stakes. Every word competes for attention from someone whose calendar is priced by the minute. Gets the main point across fast, earns the right to the detail, and makes the listener smarter rather than just informed.

## Why this matters for Cognify

Exec briefings stress **structure**, **clarity**, and **adaptability** above all. The listener won't follow a long build-up, won't tolerate hedging, and expects the speaker to have already done the discovery work. Cognify's Daily Workout and Scenario Training prompt banks use exec-briefing contexts heavily because they force the speaker to *lead with the answer* rather than *explain to it*.

## Current practitioners (2025–2026)

### Nancy Duarte — Duarte Inc.
*Presentation design firm, clients include Apple, Google, Cisco, TED. Longest-running expert in the exec-communication space.*

**Signature principle — the contrast Sparkline:** Great presentations alternate between "what is" (the status quo) and "what could be" (the new vision), ending in a call to action. The gap between them should be as big as possible — the rest of the presentation bridges that gap.

**STAR moments:** "Something They'll Always Remember." Build at least one intentional memory peak into every high-stakes presentation — a surprising stat, a physical demo, a repeatable phrase.

**Audience-first:** "Persuasion doesn't start with you and your message — it starts with your audience." The first move is understanding what they care about, not what you want to say.

### Jerry Weissman — Power Presentations
*20+ years coaching executives on their highest-stakes moments. Trained Cisco's founders for the IPO roadshow that built a $600B market cap. Author of 75 techniques for executive presentations.*

**WIIFY — "What's In It For You?"** Every presentation opens with the audience's answer, not the speaker's intro. If the first 30 seconds don't make the listener care, the rest doesn't matter.

**Verbal links** — explicit signposting between points ("The second reason I believe this…", "Which brings us to…") so the listener never has to reconstruct the argument structure.

**ERA body language** — Eye connect, Reach out, Animation. Applies to live delivery; adapts partially to voice-only training.

### Chris Orlob — pclub.io (C-suite selling focus)
*Helped grow Gong from $200K → $200M+ ARR. Specializes in enterprise / C-suite communication.*

**Core teaching — the C-suite inversion:**
- "A C-suite meeting is not a discovery call" (unless the exec came inbound)
- By the time you reach the C-suite, discovery should already be done
- Share **insights and POVs** with well-crafted questions, not discovery questions
- **"90% of sellers can't sell to the C-suite"** — the skill gap is enormous

### Carmine Gallo — communication coach, Forbes/Harvard Business Review
*Author of* Talk Like TED *and* The Presentation Secrets of Steve Jobs. *Analyzed 500+ TED talks to extract delivery patterns.*

**The 18-minute rule** — borrowed from TED. Beyond 18 minutes, cognitive load degrades sharply. Exec briefings should be *far* shorter — 5 minutes is the working ceiling; 60 seconds is the real skill.

**Three-act structure** — beginning (hook + promise), middle (supporting points, max 3), end (call to action + memorable line).

## Core principles — synthesized across sources

1. **Answer first.** Minto, Weissman, Orlob, and every modern practitioner agree. Don't build to the conclusion — start with it, then justify.
2. **One big idea.** Chris Anderson's "through-line." If you can't state the presentation in one sentence, it isn't ready.
3. **3–5 supporting points, max.** Beyond five, retention collapses. Beyond three, executives stop tracking.
4. **Quantified results, not adjectives.** "Significant improvement" is invisible. "Cut onboarding from 7 months to 3" is memorable.
5. **Audience-centric opening.** WIIFY, not "let me walk you through what we've been working on."
6. **Anticipate the objection.** Great execs will challenge the claim — have the defense already loaded.

## Common failure modes

- **Preamble and throat-clearing** — "So, um, I just wanted to share a quick update on…"
- **Chronological structure** — telling the story in the order it happened instead of in order of importance
- **Data dump without interpretation** — "here are the numbers" without "here's what they mean"
- **Burying the ask** — not stating explicitly what you need from the room
- **Hedging** — "we think maybe we should consider potentially…"
- **Marketing adjectives** — "world-class", "leading", "best-in-class", "game-changing"
- **Missing the through-line** — jumping between unrelated points with no frame

## Exemplar phrasings

- **WIIFY opener**: "I'm here because we have a shot at cutting enterprise-sales ramp time from seven months to three — and I need your help with one decision to make that real."
- **Answer-first**: "We should build a 30-day scenario bootcamp for new AEs. Three reasons. First…"
- **Quantified close**: "If we approve this in the next two weeks, new reps hired in Q3 hit quota in January instead of May. That's $2.1M in pipeline we don't currently have."
- **Verbal link**: "That's the *what*. Here's the *how*."

## Antipatterns to flag in scoring

- "I'd like to walk you through…"
- "Just wanted to share…"
- "Before I dive in, a little background…"
- "Let me take a step back…"
- "At a high level…"
- Opening with your name and team (the exec already has the calendar invite)
