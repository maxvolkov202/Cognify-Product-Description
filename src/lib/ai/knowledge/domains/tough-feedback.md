# Domain: Tough Feedback & Performance Conversations

Giving corrective or critical feedback to a peer, report, or manager in a way that actually lands — without triggering defensiveness, damaging the relationship, or softening the message into uselessness. The hardest routine communication most professionals ever do.

## Why this matters for Cognify

Feedback conversations stress **clarity**, **structure**, **adaptability**, and **handling pressure** simultaneously. They demand specificity under emotional weight — the speaker has to be concrete enough to be actionable while warm enough to preserve the relationship. Cognify's Scenario Training mode treats feedback delivery as a first-tier domain because it is the rep most professionals avoid practicing and most need to get right.

## Current practitioners (2020–2026)

### Kim Scott — Radical Candor
*Former Google and Apple executive. Her framework has become the default vocabulary for modern feedback conversations.*

**The Radical Candor 2×2:**

```
                 Care Personally →
                 LOW          HIGH
Challenge       ─────────────────────
Directly        │ Obnoxious │ Radical │
↑ HIGH          │ Aggression│  Candor │
                ├───────────┼─────────┤
                │Manipulative│Ruinous │
  LOW           │ Insincerity│ Empathy│
                └───────────┴─────────┘
```

Four quadrants:
- **Radical Candor** (target): high care + high challenge — specific and sincere praise, kind and clear criticism
- **Ruinous Empathy**: caring so much about not hurting feelings that you withhold information the person needs — the most common failure mode among new managers
- **Obnoxious Aggression**: challenging directly without showing care — "brilliant jerk" territory, destroys trust
- **Manipulative Insincerity**: neither caring nor challenging — politics and passive-aggression

**Principles:**
- **Solicit first, then give.** Ask for feedback before giving it; models vulnerability and earns permission.
- **Praise in public, criticize in private.** Public praise reinforces and builds safety; private criticism preserves dignity.
- **Be specific and sincere.** Generic praise ("great job!") rings hollow; generic criticism ("you need to improve") is not actionable.
- **Give it quickly.** Waiting weeks turns a small fixable moment into a performance issue.

### Douglas Stone & Sheila Heen — Harvard Negotiation Project
*Authors of* Thanks for the Feedback. *Focus on the receiver side of feedback — what makes it possible to hear it.*

**The three triggers that shut down feedback reception:**

1. **Truth triggers** — "That's not accurate" / "That's not fair." Receiver rejects the content.
2. **Relationship triggers** — "I can't believe *you* of all people are telling me this." Receiver rejects the giver.
3. **Identity triggers** — "This means I'm a bad person / a failure / not who I thought I was." Receiver rejects the implication.

When giving feedback, anticipate which trigger is likely — and structure the delivery to defuse it before it fires.

### Marshall Goldsmith — executive coaching
*Coached 150+ Fortune 500 CEOs. Pioneered "feedforward."*

**Feedforward instead of feedback:** Ask the person what they'd like to improve, then give concrete suggestions for *the future* rather than critiquing *the past*. Past-focused feedback triggers defense; future-focused suggestions trigger exploration. Does not replace corrective feedback but complements it.

## Core principles — synthesized across sources

1. **Observed behavior, not interpreted character.** "You interrupted her twice" (observable) ≠ "you're dismissive" (character judgment).
2. **Impact over intent.** What actually happened as a result of the behavior, not what the speaker meant.
3. **Specific and recent.** "In Wednesday's standup" beats "you tend to…". Recency matters because it's verifiable.
4. **Future-forward ask.** Every feedback conversation ends with a specific, checkable behavior change.
5. **Curiosity before conclusion.** Ask what was going on before you announce what went wrong — you may learn something that reframes the whole thing.
6. **Private, synchronous, voice (not text).** Tough feedback in Slack creates re-reading and escalation.

## Common failure modes

- **Sandwich feedback** (praise → criticism → praise) — well-researched as counterproductive; receivers discount the praise and miss the criticism. Kim Scott, Stone & Heen, and Scott Berkun all recommend dropping it.
- **Generalizing** — "you always…" / "you never…". One counterexample and the conversation is over.
- **Softening to the point of invisibility** — "it's not a big deal, but…", "this is just a small thing…". The listener concludes it wasn't important and nothing changes.
- **Ruinous empathy** — withholding because you care. Kim Scott: the kindest thing is often the hardest.
- **Public delivery** — triggers shame, not learning.
- **Text delivery** — strips tone, invites worst-case reading.
- **Ambiguous ask** — "try to be more collaborative" is unmeasurable.

## Exemplar phrasings

- **Observable opener**: "In Wednesday's standup, you interrupted Priya twice while she was explaining the cache issue."
- **Impact**: "She stopped contributing for the rest of the meeting, and we didn't hear her caching concern until after the decision was made — which we've now had to reopen."
- **Future ask**: "Going forward, I need you to let whoever is speaking finish before you respond, even if you disagree."
- **Soliciting first**: "Before I share this, is there anything you'd want *me* to change about how I'm working with you?"
- **Curiosity**: "Help me understand what was happening for you in that moment."

## Antipatterns to flag in scoring

- "I just wanted to say…"
- "This isn't a big deal, but…"
- "Some people feel that…" (passive voice hiding the speaker)
- "You always…" / "You never…"
- "I'm only telling you this because I care…" (doesn't earn what it claims)
- Skipping the observable behavior and leading with interpretation
