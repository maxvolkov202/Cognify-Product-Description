# Domain: Impromptu Speaking & Thinking on Your Feet

Speaking when you didn't know you'd be speaking. The hallway question, the meeting follow-up, the interview curveball, the toast you weren't told you'd give. Impromptu speaking is the purest test of structured thinking under pressure — you can't memorize, you can't rehearse, and the listener is evaluating you in real time.

## Why this matters for Cognify

Impromptu is the dimension David (ex-IBM / Watson) explicitly flagged as under-trained in the April 2026 advisory meeting: *"the gap in training on how to deliver communication effectively rather than just what to say."* Cognify's Daily Workout is fundamentally an impromptu gym — every rep is a prompt the user has never seen before, with no preparation time. This domain is load-bearing for the entire product.

Stresses all six dimensions but especially **thinking on the spot**, **structure**, and **handling pressure**.

## Current practitioners (2020–2026)

### Matt Abrahams — Stanford Graduate School of Business
*Lecturer at Stanford GSB. Host of the* Think Fast, Talk Smart *podcast (one of the most-downloaded business podcasts globally). Author of* Think Faster, Talk Smarter *(2023).*

This is the modern definitive work on impromptu speaking. Abrahams' framework is science-based, built from IO psych research on speaking anxiety, working memory, and audience attention.

**The Six-Step Methodology for Speaking Spontaneously:**

1. **Manage anxiety** — reframe physiological arousal as excitement, not fear. Name it out loud ("I'm nervous because this matters") to disarm it.
2. **Maximize mediocrity** — aim for "good enough," not perfect. The pursuit of perfection is what paralyzes you. B+ beats A- that never happens.
3. **Shift your mindset** — from "what do I want to say?" to "what does my audience need to hear?"
4. **Listen** — really hear the question or the room before you start. Most spontaneous-speaking failures are misreadings of what was actually asked.
5. **Structure** — use a framework. Any framework. The list below.
6. **Focus** — on the audience and the moment, not on yourself. Self-monitoring degrades performance.

**The frameworks Abrahams teaches for impromptu:**

- **What? → So what? → Now what?** — (see `wsw.md`) — the default for "what do you think about X?"
- **Problem → Solution → Benefit** — for recommendation questions
- **Position → Action → Benefit** — a tighter variant of PSPA
- **PREP** — Point → Reason → Example → Point (see `prep.md`) — Toastmasters-derived
- **Past → Present → Future** — (see `ppf.md`) — for "tell me about yourself" and introductions

**The Mindset Shift:** *"We start by saying, 'here's what I want to say,' versus, 'what does the audience or person I'm speaking to need to hear.' That's a fundamental mind shift. If I don't understand from your perspective what's important for you, what knowledge you have now versus where I would like you to be, I can't tailor my message."*

### Toastmasters International — Table Topics
*The single most-practiced impromptu-speaking training globally. In every weekly Toastmasters meeting, members are given a random topic and must speak on it for 1–2 minutes with no preparation.*

**The Table Topics discipline:**
- 1-2 minutes maximum
- No notes
- Must have a beginning, middle, and end
- Scored on structure and clarity, not polish
- Weekly practice — the volume is the point

The PREP framework (`prep.md`) is the Toastmasters default for Table Topics because it's the easiest to hold in working memory under stress.

## Core principles — synthesized across sources

1. **A framework in your head beats a blank mind.** The single best impromptu prep is memorizing 2–3 frameworks so well that one loads automatically when you hear the question.
2. **Buy 3 seconds.** Restate the question ("So you're asking whether X"), name a value ("what I think matters most here is…"), or acknowledge tension ("that's a question with two real answers"). These are thinking-time moves that don't sound like stalling.
3. **Structure beats content.** A B+ answer with a clear framework beats an A+ answer that rambles. Listeners remember structure.
4. **Listen for the real question.** Most impromptu failures are answers to the wrong question. Before answering, briefly confirm what's being asked.
5. **Manage anxiety by naming it.** Anxiety suppressed grows; anxiety named shrinks. "This matters to me so I'm nervous" is a disarming opener.
6. **End on time.** Trailing off is the #1 way to undo a great opening. Land the ending cleanly, even if you have to cut the middle.
7. **Every framework has a restate.** PREP ends with Point, WSW with Now What, PPF with Future. Without a restate, impromptu answers feel unfinished.

## Common failure modes

- **Blank start** — silence or "um" while working memory catches up
- **No structure** — answering in whatever order things come to mind
- **Forgetting the question** — ending on a different topic than the one asked
- **Over-committing to the first sentence** — saying something you can't support
- **Hedging to protect yourself** — "I don't know if I have a strong opinion…"
- **Trailing off** — no planned ending, so the answer dribbles to a stop
- **Answering the wrong question** — misreading what was actually asked
- **Performing instead of thinking** — polished delivery of nothing
- **Over-apologizing** — "sorry, I'm not sure…" kills listener confidence before content arrives

## Exemplar phrasings

- **Restate + frame:** "That's really a question about [X]. Let me give you the shortest useful answer I have."
- **Buy time honestly:** "Give me five seconds to think about that in a useful way."
- **Name the tension:** "There's a real tradeoff here. Let me give you both sides, then my take."
- **Clean close:** "So — [restate the point]. That's my answer."

## Antipatterns to flag in scoring

- "That's a great question…" (buys no time and sounds practiced)
- "I mean…" (the #1 impromptu tell)
- "Kind of, sort of, maybe, I guess…"
- "I don't really know…"
- "Off the top of my head…" (permission to be sloppy)
- "Anyways" as a transition
- Ending with "so yeah"
