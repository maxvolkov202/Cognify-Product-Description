# Domain: Storytelling & Public Speaking

Communicating an idea to an audience through narrative rather than recitation. Every great keynote, TED talk, pitch, and memorable meeting opener is built on the same mechanics: a hook that earns attention, a structure that sustains it, and a specific human moment that makes the idea stick. This is what separates "informing" from "moving."

## Why this matters for Cognify

Storytelling stresses **structure**, **tone**, and **handling pressure** under the specific constraint that the speaker has to hold the listener's attention without interrupting to check if it's working. Cognify uses storytelling scenarios to train the hardest form of structure — one where the framework is invisible to the audience but load-bearing for the speaker.

## Staple sources

- **Robert McKee**, *Story* (1997) — the screenwriting bible; used by Pixar and Netflix story rooms. Substance, structure, style, principles.
- **Chip Heath & Dan Heath**, *Made to Stick* (2007) — the SUCCES principles: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories
- **Nancy Duarte**, *Resonate* (2010) — the contrast sparkline, alternating between "what is" and "what could be"
- **Joseph Campbell**, *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* (1949) — monomyth, the underlying shape of nearly every told story
- **Aristotle**, *Poetics* (c. 335 BCE) — ethos, pathos, logos; the original three-act structure

## Current practitioners (2020–2026)

### Chris Anderson — TED
*Curator of TED since 2001. Author of* TED Talks: The Official TED Guide to Public Speaking *(2016). Has hosted or edited more TED talks than any living human.*

**The Through-Line Principle.** Every great talk has exactly one idea — one "through-line" — that every other piece of the talk serves. If you can't state the talk in a single sentence, it isn't ready. Anderson: "A through-line is like a strong cord or rope on which you will attach all the elements of your talk."

**The 18-minute cognitive limit.** TED talks cap at 18 minutes because attention degrades sharply beyond that. Anderson argues 18 minutes is "long enough to be serious and short enough to hold people's attention," and it forces merciless pruning.

**Four approaches to the idea:** connection (build the personal bridge), narration (tell the story), explanation (build the mental model), persuasion (rearrange the audience's thinking). Every TED talk uses at least one.

### Carmine Gallo — communications coach
*Analyzed 500+ TED talks for* Talk Like TED *(2014). Follow-up books:* The Storyteller's Secret, The Bezos Blueprint.

**The Nine Principles of great TED talks:** Passion, Storytelling, Practice, the Rule of Three, Making the Audience Care, Authenticity, Visual Aids, Brevity, Enjoyment.

**Three types of stories a speaker can use:**
1. Personal stories — the most powerful; build neural coupling between speaker and listener
2. Stories about other people — useful when personal stories would feel narcissistic
3. Stories about brands or products — weakest, and nearly always replaceable by a better type

**"The human brain loves novelty."** An unfamiliar, unusual, or unexpected element in a presentation intrigues the audience and jolts them out of their preconceived notions.

**The Rule of Three.** Audiences retain three items at a time under cognitive load. Not four, not five. Three.

### Nancy Duarte — Duarte Inc.
*See also `exec-briefing.md` for her broader framework.*

**The Contrast Sparkline.** Great presentations and stories alternate between "what is" (the status quo) and "what could be" (the vision). The gap between them is the engine — the wider the gap, the more the audience cares.

**STAR moments** — "Something They'll Always Remember." Build at least one intentional peak into every talk: a surprising stat, a physical demo, a vulnerable personal moment, a repeatable phrase. Duarte's research: without a STAR moment, 90% of presentations are forgotten within 24 hours.

### Brené Brown — researcher / speaker
*Author of* Daring Greatly, Braving the Wilderness, Dare to Lead. *Her 2010 TEDx Houston talk on vulnerability is one of the most-watched TED talks of all time.*

**Vulnerability as narrative anchor.** Brown's research and speaking practice both show that listeners bond with speakers who risk being wrong, afraid, or uncertain on stage. Polished invulnerable delivery creates distance; vulnerability closes it. The risk has to be genuine — performed vulnerability registers as manipulation.

## Core principles — synthesized across sources

1. **One idea.** If the talk is about more than one thing, it's not ready.
2. **Start with the audience, not yourself.** WIIFY (What's In It For You) — not "let me tell you about what I've been working on."
3. **Contrast is the engine.** "What is" vs "what could be." Status quo vs vision. Problem vs solution. Without contrast, there's no story.
4. **Specific beats general.** One named person in one named moment beats a thousand statistics. Then pair the specific with the general.
5. **Emotion before logic, then logic.** The audience has to *feel* the point before they're ready to *think* it.
6. **Rehearse the opening and the closing.** The middle can be improvised. The first 30 seconds and the last 30 seconds can't.
7. **The rule of three.** Three supporting points, max. Three examples, max. Three memorable lines, max.
8. **Build in a STAR moment.** At least one peak the audience will still remember tomorrow.

## Common failure modes

- **Multiple through-lines** — the talk is about "innovation and culture and leadership" instead of one specific claim
- **No hook** — starting with "Hi, my name is…" instead of a compelling opening
- **Abstract opening** — talking about "the importance of communication" when you should be telling a 20-second story
- **Over-prepared delivery** — memorized to the point of sounding robotic
- **Ignoring the audience** — same talk, same slides, regardless of who's in the room
- **No emotional peak** — all logic, no human moment
- **Weak ending** — trailing off with "so… yeah, that's what I wanted to share"
- **Data without interpretation** — showing a chart without telling the audience what it means

## Exemplar phrasings

- **Hook openings:**
  - "Eight months ago I watched a friend die because his ICU nurse couldn't explain something to the attending physician clearly enough."
  - "Every morning 4 million Americans wake up and do something their grandparents would have called impossible."
  - "This is a story about a Tuesday I will never forget."
- **Contrast frame:** "Today, [X is true and broken]. Imagine instead [Y — specific, concrete, achievable]."
- **The ask close:** "If you take one thing from this: [specific repeatable line]. And tomorrow morning, do [one specific action]."

## Antipatterns to flag in scoring

- "Hi, my name is…" as the opening line
- "Today I'm going to talk about…"
- "Let me start with a little background…"
- "In conclusion…" (as the actual transition to the conclusion)
- "I'd like to thank you for having me…" as the close
- "So… yeah" as the close
- Using phrases like "amazing", "incredible", "game-changing" without the example to earn them
