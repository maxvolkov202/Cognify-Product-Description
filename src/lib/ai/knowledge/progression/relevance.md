# Progression: Relevance

Relevance measures whether the rep actually addresses the prompt. Improvements here are about *focus* — less wandering, more direct engagement with what was asked.

## What improved relevance sounds like

- **Addressed the prompt in the opening sentence** — no ramp-up, straight into the ask
- **Cut unrelated backstory** — went from 20s of context to 5s before getting to the answer
- **Every sentence earns its place** — no obvious filler or tangential anecdote
- **Answered the *actual* question** — not a nearby question the speaker preferred
- **Specific example directly supports the claim** — not a loosely related story

## What regressed relevance sounds like

- **Wandered off prompt** — started answering, drifted to tangent, forgot to land
- **Backstory creep** — more setup than answer
- **Redefined the question** — rephrased the prompt into something easier to answer
- **Generic answer that could fit any prompt** — no specific grounding in what was actually asked

## Coaching observations by delta band

| Delta | What to say |
|---|---|
| +15 or more | Quote the opening — call out how directly it hits the prompt. |
| +5 to +14 | Point at which tangent got cut. Or which sentence started earning its keep. |
| -5 to +4 | Stable. Move on. |
| -6 or worse | Quote the moment they drifted. Show exactly where focus broke. |

## Still-needs-work signals

- A middle section that's on-prompt but loosely so — example is *adjacent*, not *on-point*
- Close that ties back to the prompt but weakly ("so, like, that's my take on it")
- One unnecessary sentence still surviving — find it and name it

## Exemplar comparison phrases

- "This rep answered the prompt in the first 8 seconds. Last rep you spent 20 seconds on context before the real answer."
- "You cut the tangent about the team offsite — that saved you 15s and sharpened the throughline."
- "The middle example is directionally right but still not tight — the anecdote proves adjacent point, not *the* point."
