# Progression: Tone

Tone measures whether the register matches the audience and the stakes. Improvements here are about *calibration* — sounding like a peer when the audience is a peer, an authority when the audience is junior, etc.

## What improved tone sounds like

- **Register matches stakeholder** — CFO-facing rep sounds like someone briefing a CFO, not a colleague venting
- **Warmth without sycophancy** — acknowledged the listener without over-soothing
- **Directness without aggression** — stated the ask without softening or attacking
- **Humor / levity where appropriate** — used sparingly, landed
- **Emotional signal matches content** — serious topic sounds serious; playful topic isn't buried

## What regressed tone sounds like

- **Register drift** — started formal, drifted casual; or vice versa
- **Over-softened** — "I was just wondering if maybe" swamping what should be a direct ask
- **Over-hardened** — short, clipped, audience would feel lectured
- **Mismatched affect** — cheery about a serious topic, or vice versa

## Coaching observations by delta band

| Delta | What to say |
|---|---|
| +15 or more | Name the specific register shift. Quote a moment where the tone matched exactly. |
| +5 to +14 | One or two moments where the register landed better. Point to them. |
| -5 to +4 | Stable. Redirect. |
| -6 or worse | Name the specific tone mismatch. Too soft? Too blunt? Wrong register? |

## Still-needs-work signals

- One soft-opener that undermines an otherwise-calibrated rep ("so, not to bother you, but…")
- An over-casual aside in an otherwise-formal rep
- Closing line that drops the calibrated tone ("thanks so much, sorry again!")

## Exemplar comparison phrases

- "The opener 'here's the call we're making' is exactly the right register for a board update. Last rep started 'so, wanted to flag something' — same content, wrong stakes."
- "You stayed peer-to-peer the whole rep — no dipping into apology, no pivoting into lecture."
- "Most of the rep lands at the right register, but the close ('sorry this was long, thanks!') steps out of it. Trim it."
