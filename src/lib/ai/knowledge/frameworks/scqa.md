# Framework: Situation → Complication → Question → Answer (SCQA)

Consulting-grade framing for executive audiences. Developed by **Barbara Minto** at McKinsey in the 1960s and published in *The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking* (1987). The cornerstone of modern management consulting communication.

## When to use

- Strategy recommendations
- Executive briefings
- Cross-functional pitches where "why does this matter" must land before "what do we do"
- Any moment the listener needs to *feel the tension* before they hear your answer

## The nodes

1. **Situation** — where things stand today. The facts your audience already accepts. This is common ground, not news.
2. **Complication** — what changed, what's at risk, what's broken. This is where tension enters.
3. **Question** — the implicit question the complication has created in the listener's mind. You name it so they don't have to form it themselves.
4. **Answer** — your recommendation. The top of your pyramid.

## Exemplar phrasing

> "Our enterprise sales team has been closing 40% of qualified leads for three quarters. [**Situation**] But our AE onboarding time has doubled since we shifted to complex-sale territory — new reps take seven months to hit quota instead of three. [**Complication**] So how do we get new hires to full productivity faster without sacrificing rigor? [**Question**] We should build a 30-day scenario-training bootcamp modeled on what product engineers already do with code-review bootcamps. [**Answer**]"

## Common failure modes

- **Situation bloat** — 60 seconds of common ground no one needs. Execs already know it. Trim.
- **Weak complication** — the audience doesn't feel the tension, so the answer feels unearned. If there's no complication, there's no reason for the recommendation.
- **Missing Question** — jumping from complication to answer without naming the question forces the listener to construct it themselves, and they often construct a different one than you're answering.
- **Hedged answer** — "we could potentially explore considering…". SCQA demands a real answer.

## Antipatterns

- Starting with the answer (that's Minto Pyramid — use `minto.md` when urgency > tension)
- Letting the complication drift into a list of problems instead of one sharp tension
- Treating SCQA as writing-only — it works live, but demands real discipline on Situation length

## Origin

Barbara Minto, *The Pyramid Principle* (1987). Minto was McKinsey's first woman consultant. SCQA inverts how most people naturally communicate — instead of building *to* a conclusion, you lead with the tension that earns it, then deliver the conclusion.
