# Framework: Problem → Solution → Proof → Ask (PSPA)

Modern B2B sales framing. Pain → offer → evidence → next step. Derived from Neil Rackham's SPIN Selling and Matthew Dixon's Challenger Sale, condensed into four nodes for live delivery in cold calls, demos, and discovery rooms.

## When to use

- Cold call value statements (compressed, under 30 seconds)
- Product demos (2–5 minutes, full form)
- Discovery calls closing into a next step
- Any internal pitch where you need the listener to commit to a follow-up
- Investor updates where traction is the ask

## The nodes

1. **Problem** — the specific pain the buyer is feeling today. Not your product's feature list. The *feeling* on their side of the table.
2. **Solution** — how your thing addresses it. Specific, not generic. "We reduce onboarding time from 7 months to 3" beats "we make onboarding easier."
3. **Proof** — evidence it works. A case, a number, a demo moment, a quote. Not a logo wall.
4. **Ask** — the specific next step you want. Not "let me know if you're interested." A calendar time, a trial, a referral.

## Exemplar phrasing

**Compressed — cold call opener:**
> "Most enterprise AEs I talk to lose 4–5 months of ramp because they don't practice complex-sale scenarios before they're live in front of customers. [**Problem**] We built a scenario gym where they run ten reps a day on the real conversations they'll have — discovery, objection handling, executive briefings — and get scored on it. [**Solution**] At Datadog, new hires using this flow hit quota in their fourth month instead of the seventh. [**Proof**] Would Tuesday or Thursday work for a 15-minute walkthrough? [**Ask**]"

**Full form — demo close:**
> "The problem we solve is the gap between what courses teach and what actually happens in a live sales call. [**Problem**] Cognify generates a framework for the conversation, records the rep's attempt, scores it against the framework, and shows improvement over time. [**Solution**] One of our pilot customers cut new-AE ramp from seven months to four, and their blind-listener rankings showed a 60% improvement after 20 reps. [**Proof**] I'd like to set up a two-week pilot with your SDR team — no cost, no contract. Can we schedule a kickoff for next Wednesday? [**Ask**]"

## Why the Ask is non-negotiable

Every experienced sales trainer (Dixon, Rackham, Dan Pink, Chris Orlob) agrees: without a specific Ask, PSPA is a status update, not a sale. "Let me know if you're interested" puts the cognitive burden on the buyer and they will not carry it. The specific Ask ("Tuesday or Thursday?") puts the burden on the *speaker* and that's where it belongs.

## Common failure modes

- **Problem as feature dressed up** — "the problem is that you don't have our thing yet" (Matthew Dixon calls this "commission breath")
- **Generic Solution** — "we help companies scale" tells the listener nothing
- **Logo-wall Proof** — "we work with Google, Apple, and Microsoft" is not proof of *your specific claim*. Specific before famous.
- **Soft Ask** — "let me know if this sounds interesting" is not an ask
- **Skipping Problem** — leading with Solution. The listener has no reason to care yet.
- **Over-quantifying Proof** — buyers discount any number that feels too round or too perfect

## Antipatterns

- "I just wanted to quickly share…"
- "We're a cloud-based end-to-end…"
- "Let me know if you'd like to hear more…"
- "We help companies be more efficient…"
- "Feel free to reach out if this resonates…"

## PSPA in Cognify's scoring

Cold call and sales scenario reps using PSPA are scored on **all four nodes landed in order**. Missing the Ask is the most common failure and is flagged as a structural failure, not a delivery failure — it's a thinking error, not a word-choice error.

## Origin

Synthesized from:
- **Neil Rackham**, *SPIN Selling* (1988) — Situation/Problem/Implication/Need-payoff
- **Matthew Dixon**, *The Challenger Sale* (2011) — teach-tailor-take control
- **Daniel Pink**, *To Sell Is Human* (2012) — clarity, attunement, buoyancy
- **Chris Orlob**, pclub.io training — modern enterprise SaaS framing

The PSPA condensation is not attributed to a single author; it is the common shape that modern sales trainers converge on when teaching live-delivery framing.
