# Framework: Answer → Reasons → Evidence (Minto Pyramid)

The short-form variant of Barbara Minto's Pyramid Principle. Lead with the answer, then justify it. The opposite of SCQA — use Minto when **urgency beats tension**, when the audience wants the punchline *now* and will drill in on demand.

## When to use

- Executive emails ("what did we decide and why?")
- The first 30 seconds of any senior meeting
- Status updates to a busy stakeholder
- Any moment the listener will interrupt you if you take too long to land the point
- Live Q&A under pressure

## The nodes

1. **Answer** — your conclusion. No preamble, no lead-up, no "so the way I see it…". One sentence. The top of the pyramid.
2. **Reasons** — three supporting reasons, mutually exclusive. Not a list of everything you considered — the three that *earn* the answer.
3. **Evidence** — the data, logic, or example behind each reason. Only delivered if the listener drills in. In written form, Evidence lives in the paragraph under each reason. In spoken form, it lives in your head, ready to deploy.

## Exemplar phrasing

**Strong:**
> "We should sunset the mobile app and push web-first. [**Answer**] Three reasons. First, our mobile install base has declined 40% year over year. Second, the engineering cost of maintaining two codebases is consuming 30% of our frontend capacity. Third, our web conversion has now overtaken mobile on every metric we track. [**Reasons**] Happy to go deeper on any of those. [**Evidence on deck**]"

## Why three

Three is not arbitrary. Two reasons feels thin and hedgeable. Four or more and the listener stops tracking. The Heath Brothers (Made to Stick) and Minto both land on three as the cognitive sweet spot for structured reasoning under time pressure.

The three reasons must be **mutually exclusive** — if two reasons overlap, they collapse into one and the structure falls apart. "Cost, speed, and efficiency" is two reasons at most (speed and efficiency overlap).

## Common failure modes

- **Burying the answer** — "so there are a few things to consider, and I've been thinking about this…" *That's not Minto.* Minto opens with the answer.
- **Three overlapping reasons** — fails the mutually-exclusive test. Collapse them into distinct ones.
- **Evidence-first delivery** — starting with the data and building to the answer. That's the opposite of Minto. In exec communication, the listener has already decided whether to keep listening by the time you finish the data.
- **More than three reasons** — attention breaks. Pick the three strongest and cut the rest.

## Antipatterns

- "Well, there are several factors at play here…"
- "Let me give you some context first…"
- "I've been thinking about this a lot and I think…"
- Ending without offering the Evidence on deck

## When NOT to use Minto

Use **SCQA** (`scqa.md`) when the audience doesn't yet feel the tension — when you need them to *want* the answer before you give it. Minto assumes the listener already wants the answer and just needs it delivered.

Use **BLUF** (`bluf.md`) when you have under 60 seconds and three reasons won't fit.

## Origin

Barbara Minto, *The Pyramid Principle: Logic in Writing and Thinking* (1987). Minto was McKinsey's first woman consultant and built the pyramid principle during the 1960s while training new consultants to write client memos. The Answer-first form became McKinsey's house style and spread through consulting into exec comms broadly.
