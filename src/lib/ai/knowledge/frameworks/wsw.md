# Framework: What → So What → Now What (WSW)

Reflective practice framework. Turns observation into action. Developed by John Driscoll in the 1990s for nursing reflection; adopted into management training, retrospectives, and impromptu speaking.

Matt Abrahams (Stanford, *Think Faster, Talk Smarter*) recommends WSW as the default impromptu-speaking framework for "what do you think about X?" questions — it's specifically engineered to load fast into working memory under pressure.

## When to use

- Retrospectives and post-mortems
- Debriefs after a meeting, pitch, or event
- Reflective moments in performance reviews
- **Impromptu "what do you think?" questions** — WSW is Abrahams' go-to framework here
- Journal entries and learning reviews
- Any moment you need to turn an observation into a commitment

## The nodes

1. **What** — what happened. Objectively. Facts, not feelings. The observable events.
2. **So What** — what it means. Why it matters. The interpretation layer — this is where you add judgment.
3. **Now What** — what changes as a result. The concrete next action. The commitment.

## Why Now What is load-bearing

WSW distinguishes itself from most reflection frameworks because the *Now What* node is non-optional. Reflection without action is just rumination. The Now What is what converts the insight into movement — and in impromptu speaking, it's the node that makes the answer feel *complete* rather than just *thoughtful*.

Without Now What, WSW collapses into "I saw a thing and here's what I think." With it, WSW becomes a closed loop: observation → interpretation → commitment.

## Exemplar phrasing

**Retrospective:**
> "Our cycle time on the last sprint went from 6 days average to 11 days. [**What**] The slowdown was concentrated in code review — we had two senior engineers on vacation simultaneously and the remaining reviewers got bottlenecked. [**So What**] Going forward, we're going to enforce that no more than one senior engineer per code-owner group is out at the same time, and we're training two mid-levels to be secondary approvers on the critical paths. [**Now What**]"

**Impromptu "what do you think about X?" — Abrahams' recommended use:**
> "The big shift I'm seeing is that AI tools are collapsing the gap between junior and senior output quality. [**What**] That matters because the training pipeline for junior hires is built around a gap that's no longer there — we used to teach juniors by giving them work that AI now does in 10 minutes. [**So What**] So I think the companies that win the next five years are going to be the ones that completely redesign junior onboarding around judgment and context, not around output. [**Now What**]"

## Common failure modes

- **Interpreting in the What node** — mixing observation with judgment. What should be purely observable; So What is where interpretation belongs.
- **Skipping Now What** — ending at So What and leaving the reflection dangling. Without action, there's no framework — just musing.
- **Generic Now What** — "we should do better next time" is not a Now What. A specific, named, checkable action is.
- **Emotional What** — "it was really frustrating when…" is a feeling, not an observation
- **Over-generalizing from a single instance** — WSW is for reflection, not theorizing; keep the scope tight

## Antipatterns

- "What I noticed was that people were kind of, like, frustrated…" (interpretation + hedging in the What node)
- "So what we probably should think about doing…" (diluting the Now What into vapor)
- "I think we just need to be better at communicating…" (unmeasurable Now What)
- Ending with "…so yeah, that's kind of where I'm at"

## WSW vs similar frameworks

- **Driscoll's original 3-questions** (What? / So What? / Now What?) — the source material. Identical structure; WSW is the acronym form.
- **Gibbs' Reflective Cycle** — 6-node version with more structure. Useful for deep retrospective; too heavy for impromptu.
- **PREP** (`prep.md`) — when the question is not reflective but opinion-based
- **STAR** (`star.md`) — when the question is "tell me about a time"

## Origin

John Driscoll, "Reflective practice for practise," *Senior Nurse*, 1994. Driscoll adapted philosopher Terry Borton's "What? So What? Now What?" model (1970) into a clinical reflection framework for nursing practice. The 3-question form spread through healthcare education, management training, and impromptu-speaking methodology over the following two decades.

Matt Abrahams (Stanford GSB) reintroduced WSW to a business-communication audience in his 2023 book *Think Faster, Talk Smarter*, specifically recommending it as the default impromptu-speaking framework for opinion and reflection questions.
