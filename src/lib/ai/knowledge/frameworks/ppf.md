# Framework: Past → Present → Future (PPF)

The classic narrative structure for self-introductions and career framing. The default framework for "tell me about yourself" and the cleanest way to make a personal story feel structured without feeling rehearsed.

## When to use

- Interview openers ("tell me about yourself")
- Self-introductions in a new room
- Networking events / mixers
- LinkedIn About sections
- Conference speaker intros
- Any moment you're asked to summarize your career in under 90 seconds

## The nodes

1. **Past** — one line of *relevant* history. Not your full biography. Just enough to establish the foundation for Present.
2. **Present** — what you're doing *right now* that connects to this moment. The answer to "why are we talking?"
3. **Future** — where you're heading and why *this* conversation matters to that direction.

## Why the Future node is load-bearing

Most people stop at Present. They say where they came from and where they are now and leave the listener wondering "…and?" The Future node is what makes the introduction feel purposeful rather than retrospective. It also does the meta-work of *why are we in this conversation* without forcing the listener to construct it.

In an interview context, the Future node should land *exactly* on the role you're interviewing for. That's not coincidence — it's structure.

## Exemplar phrasing

**Strong (interview opener):**
> "I started my career as a data engineer at Spotify, building the pipeline that still powers their Wrapped campaigns. [**Past**] For the last two years I've been leading infrastructure at a 40-person ML startup, where I rebuilt our training pipeline from the ground up and cut our weekly GPU spend by 60%. [**Present**] What I'm looking for next is a role where I can take that experience and apply it at a bigger scale — specifically, the kind of work your platform team is doing on multi-tenant GPU scheduling. [**Future**]"

**Strong (networking mixer):**
> "I was a theater major who ended up in product marketing. [**Past**] Right now I run B2B marketing at a communication-training startup called Cognify. [**Present**] I'm here tonight because we're thinking about our first enterprise L&D pilots and I want to meet people who've done that before. [**Future**]"

## Common failure modes

- **Past bloat** — 60 seconds of career history that doesn't lead anywhere. The Past node should be one sentence, maybe two.
- **Irrelevant Past** — details about where you grew up or what you studied when they have no bearing on the present conversation
- **Missing Future** — trailing off at Present, leaving the listener to connect the dots
- **Generic Future** — "I'm looking for the next chapter of my career" is not a Future. Specific role, specific ambition, specific bridge to *this* conversation.
- **Chronology trap** — telling the career in strict chronological order instead of in importance order
- **Tell-me-about-yourself as autobiography** — treating the question as an invitation to narrate your life

## Antipatterns

- "So I was born in…"
- "I went to college at…"
- "And then I worked at X, then Y, then Z…" (pure chronology)
- "I've done a lot of different things…"
- "I don't really know where to start…"

## PPF vs STAR

PPF is for **introductions** and career framing — the first 60 seconds of a conversation.

STAR (`star.md`) is for **behavioral questions** — specific stories about specific situations.

A strong interview uses both: PPF opens the conversation, STAR runs every behavioral question that follows.

## Origin

Long-standing narrative pattern in rhetoric and career counseling. The three-act structure (past/present/future) predates formal frameworks but was codified for business communication in the 1990s by career coaches and outplacement firms. Also deeply rooted in Aristotle's dramatic unity — every Western narrative has some version of before/during/after.
