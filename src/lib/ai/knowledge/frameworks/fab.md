# Framework: Feature → Advantage → Benefit (FAB)

Classic sales framework. Translates "what the thing does" into "why the buyer should care." The simplest way to move from mechanics to meaning in a product pitch.

## When to use

- Product demos (especially feature walkthroughs)
- Explaining a technical capability to a non-technical buyer
- Answering "what does your product do?" in 20 seconds
- Any moment you're describing a capability and need the listener to *feel* the value, not just understand the mechanism

## The nodes

1. **Feature** — what the thing actually is. The mechanic. *"Cognify generates a framework for every scenario."*
2. **Advantage** — what it does *better* than the alternative. The comparison. *"Unlike a static script, the framework adapts to the audience and constraints the user types in."*
3. **Benefit** — what it *means* for the buyer's life or work. The outcome. *"So an AE walking into a real call already has a structured way to think about it, not a memorized script that breaks the moment the customer goes off-topic."*

## Exemplar phrasing

**Strong:**
> "[Feature] Cognify's scoring pipeline runs deterministic signal extraction on every rep — filler rate, pacing, pauses — alongside the LLM layer. [Advantage] That means our trend lines are stable across time in a way Yoodli's and Speeko's aren't — re-scoring yesterday's audio returns the same number today. [Benefit] Your L&D team can report real improvement to the CFO with numbers that hold up under audit, not vibes that drift every release."

## Why the Benefit node is load-bearing

Most salespeople stop at Advantage. They describe what's better and assume the buyer will infer the "so what?" They don't. The Benefit node is the buyer's *so what*, translated into their language, their KPI, their boss's question. Without Benefit, the buyer hears "this is technically interesting" and forgets.

The Benefit also has to match the specific buyer's world. "Saves time" is not a benefit — "cuts your monthly calibration review from 4 hours to 30 minutes" is. The Benefit is concrete or it's nothing.

## Common failure modes

- **Feature dumping** — listing 6 features without connecting to Advantage or Benefit
- **Advantage without Benefit** — "it's faster / better / more accurate" without the "which means for you…"
- **Generic Benefit** — "saves time", "drives efficiency", "improves outcomes" — these are not benefits, they are the *category* of benefits
- **Buyer-mismatched Benefit** — describing the benefit to the engineer when you're pitching the CFO
- **Feature dressed as Benefit** — "the benefit is that you get our AI-powered platform" — that's still a feature

## Antipatterns

- "It comes with…"
- "It's powered by…"
- "It's best-in-class at…"
- "The main differentiator is…" (then describes a feature)
- Bullet-list delivery ("First, it does X. Second, it does Y. Third, it does Z.")

## FAB vs PSPA

Use **FAB** when the buyer has already agreed there's a problem and you're now showing the product's mechanics. FAB is *feature-centric*.

Use **PSPA** (`pspa.md`) when the buyer doesn't yet agree there's a problem, or when you're opening the conversation. PSPA is *problem-centric*.

In a full sales cycle: PSPA runs the discovery and pitch; FAB runs the demo and feature drill-down.

## Origin

Attributed to Xerox PARC's sales training programs in the 1960s, later systematized by Dale Carnegie Training and mainstreamed into enterprise sales curricula by the 1980s. FAB is the *oldest* modern sales framework still in wide use — older than SPIN (Rackham, 1988) and older than the Challenger Sale (Dixon, 2011).
