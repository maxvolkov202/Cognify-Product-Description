# Framework: Bottom Line → Context → Next Step (BLUF)

Ruthless compression. Originally US military communication doctrine, now standard in exec email subject lines and live 60-second updates. Use when you have less time than you'd like and the listener has less patience than you'd hope.

## When to use

- Emails to executives (subject line + first sentence)
- 60-second hallway updates
- Status updates in a meeting you're not on the agenda for
- Slack messages to a VP
- Any moment the listener will re-read this later in a hurry

## The nodes

1. **Bottom Line** — the one sentence they need to hear. If they read only this sentence, they know what they need to do.
2. **Context** — the minimum background to make the bottom line make sense. One sentence, two at the most.
3. **Next Step** — what you need from them, or what happens next.

## Exemplar phrasings

**Slack to a VP:**
> "**Bottom line:** the migration is going to slip two weeks. **Context:** the data validation step is uncovering corruption we didn't see in staging. **Next step:** I need you to push the investor demo from the 18th to the 25th. I can draft the note to the board if you want."

**Email subject + opening:**
> **Subject:** Migration slipping 2 weeks — need board date pushed
>
> Data validation is uncovering corruption we didn't catch in staging, so the migration will land the 25th instead of the 11th. I need the board demo pushed to match. Draft note attached for your review.

## Why BLUF works

Executives scan. They read subject lines, opening sentences, and Ctrl-F for their name. BLUF is engineered to survive that reading pattern: every key piece of information is where a scanning reader would find it.

BLUF also **protects the sender** — you can't be accused of "burying the bad news" if the bad news is in sentence one.

## Common failure modes

- **Throat-clearing opener** — "Hi [Name], hope you're doing well. Just wanted to share a quick update on…" — BLUF has no greeting-as-preamble. The bottom line comes first.
- **Vague bottom line** — "There's been some movement on the migration project" is not a bottom line. *"Migration slipping two weeks"* is.
- **Next step missing** — ending at Context. If BLUF doesn't tell the reader what to do with the information, it's just a status report.
- **Over-contextualizing** — when Context runs longer than Bottom Line + Next Step combined, the shape is broken. Cut the context in half.

## Antipatterns

- "I just wanted to give you a quick heads up on something…"
- "I know you're busy so I'll make this quick, but…"
- "Long story short…" (then tells the long story)
- "Before I get into the details…"

## BLUF vs Minto vs SCQA

- **BLUF** when you have ≤ 60 seconds or ≤ 3 sentences — maximum compression, minimum structure
- **Minto** when you have 2–5 minutes — Answer + three Reasons, full drill-in on request
- **SCQA** when the listener doesn't yet feel the tension and you need them to want the answer

## Origin

US military communication doctrine. The term "Bottom Line Up Front" dates to military intelligence briefings in the mid-20th century, designed for commanders who read only the first paragraph of any report and act on it. Adopted by management consulting in the 1990s and mainstreamed into exec email norms by the 2000s.
