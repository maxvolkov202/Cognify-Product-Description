# Framework: Attention → Interest → Desire → Action (AIDA)

The oldest persuasion framework still in wide use. Elias St. Elmo Lewis, 1898. Designed for audiences who don't yet know they have a problem and won't read a second sentence unless the first one earned it.

## When to use

- Cold audiences — people who didn't come to listen to you
- Opening talks at conferences
- Marketing copy and landing pages
- Any moment you have to *earn* attention before you have trust
- Pitch openers where the listener's default is to tune out

## The nodes

1. **Attention** — the hook that makes them stop. A surprising statistic, a personal story opener, a physical demo, a provocative question. 5–15 seconds.
2. **Interest** — the reason they should keep listening. Why this matters to them, specifically. 15–30 seconds.
3. **Desire** — why they'd want this to be true *for them*. The emotional / aspirational pull. 30–60 seconds.
4. **Action** — the specific thing you want them to do next. One move, clearly named.

## Exemplar phrasing

**Strong (conference opener):**
> "In the next 60 seconds, three people in this room will decide what to wear tomorrow and forget every slide I'm about to show you. [**Attention**] That's not because what I'm going to say isn't important — it's because the human brain is literally designed to forget structured information within hours unless it's attached to a story. [**Interest**] Imagine you're running a sales team where every rep remembers the framework you taught them six months later, not because they wrote it down, but because it's muscle memory. [**Desire**] I'm going to spend the next 10 minutes showing you exactly how we built that, and I'll send everyone in this room the framework if you put your business card in the box by the door. [**Action**]"

## Why Attention is the hardest node

By far the most common failure in AIDA is spending 90% of your effort on Interest and Desire and treating Attention as a throwaway greeting. But if Attention fails, the rest of the framework never runs — the listener already tuned out.

Great Attention hooks are **specific**, **unexpected**, and **relevant in under 10 seconds**. Vague, expected, or abstract openings lose the listener before you've said anything worth hearing.

## Common failure modes

- **Greeting as Attention** — "Hi, I'm excited to be here today" is not an attention hook
- **Attention without Interest bridge** — the hook lands but doesn't connect to anything the audience cares about
- **Skipping to Action** — asking for the commitment before building Desire
- **Interest that's about you, not them** — "let me tell you about our amazing team" instead of "here's why this matters for you"
- **Desire that's generic** — "imagine a better future" instead of "imagine your CFO thanking you for cutting $400K from the training budget"
- **Vague Action** — "reach out if you're interested" is not an action

## Antipatterns

- "Thanks for having me, it's great to be here today…"
- "Before we get started, a little about me…"
- "Today we're going to be talking about…"
- "Let me show you our platform…"
- "Feel free to reach out if you'd like to learn more…"

## AIDA vs modern alternatives

AIDA is designed for *cold* audiences. For warm audiences — people who asked for the meeting — use:

- **Minto Pyramid** (`minto.md`) when the listener already wants the answer
- **SCQA** (`scqa.md`) when the listener knows the area but doesn't yet feel the tension
- **PSPA** (`pspa.md`) when the listener is already engaged and you're making a specific ask

AIDA remains useful in 2026 for its original purpose: situations where you have to *win* the listener's attention before you can inform, persuade, or ask.

## Origin

Elias St. Elmo Lewis, advertising pioneer, 1898. Originally formulated for print advertising but has been adapted into sales, speech, copywriting, and presentation methodology continuously for 128 years. The durability comes from the fact that attention is still the first constraint in any form of communication — and probably always will be.
