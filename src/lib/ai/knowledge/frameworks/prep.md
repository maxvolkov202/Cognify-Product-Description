# Framework: Point → Reason → Example → Point (PREP)

The impromptu-speaking scaffold from Toastmasters International. The default framework for answering an unexpected question with structure but zero planning time.

## When to use

- Answering a question you weren't expecting ("what do you think about X?")
- Group discussions where you have 10 seconds to organize a response
- Quick interview follow-ups
- Any moment you need to sound prepared without being prepared

## The nodes

1. **Point** — state your point directly. No windup, no "well, that's a great question…", no preamble.
2. **Reason** — one sentence on *why* you believe it.
3. **Example** — a concrete case that proves it. Specific, recent, real.
4. **Point (restated)** — repeat your point, tighter than the first time. This is the frame that makes PREP feel complete instead of trailing off.

## Why the restate matters

The restated Point is what separates PREP from PRE. Without it, the response feels unfinished. The restate is ≤ 50% the length of the original point, and it often reframes slightly — the example has earned you the right to say the point more forcefully the second time.

## Exemplar phrasing

> "**Remote work should default to async, not sync.** [Point] Every sync meeting is a tax on everyone's focus time, and most of what happens in them could be a written update. [Reason] On my last team, we killed half our standing meetings and replaced them with written updates — cycle time on the main product dropped 30% within a quarter. [Example] So yes — async by default, sync by exception. [Point restated]"

## Common failure modes

- **Burying the Point** — starting with "so I think there's kind of like a few things to consider…". PREP demands the Point comes first.
- **Abstract Example** — "you see this in a lot of companies" is not an example. A company name, a number, a moment — that's an example.
- **Missing the restate** — trailing off after the example feels unfinished. The restate lands the plane.
- **Over-reasoning** — a paragraph-long Reason loses the punch. One sentence.

## Antipatterns

- "Well, it's a great question…"
- "There's a few ways to look at it…"
- "I don't know if I have a strong opinion, but…"
- Hedging the point ("I kind of think maybe that…")

## Origin

Toastmasters International impromptu-speaking methodology. Used in Toastmasters "Table Topics" sessions since the 1950s to teach extemporaneous structure. Variants (PREEP, 5Ps) exist but the PREP form is the most widely-taught.
