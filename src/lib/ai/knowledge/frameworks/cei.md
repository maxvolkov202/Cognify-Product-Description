# Framework: Claim → Evidence → Implication (CEI)

The backbone of any argument that could be challenged. Adapted from classical Aristotelian logos for modern business communication. Use CEI when you expect pushback, when the claim is contested, or when the listener will reasonably ask "why should I believe you?"

## When to use

- Debates and defenses of a recommendation
- Persuasive presentations where the audience starts skeptical
- Performance review self-assessments ("here's what I did and why it mattered")
- Any moment your claim needs to survive being questioned
- Academic, legal, or scientific argumentation in business form

## The nodes

1. **Claim** — the assertion you're making. Clear, falsifiable, worth defending. Not a question, not a hedge.
2. **Evidence** — data, reasoning, or a specific example that supports the claim. Real. Cited. Checkable.
3. **Implication** — why the claim *matters* to your audience specifically. This is the "so what" that converts logic into commitment.

## Exemplar phrasing

**Strong:**
> "Our scoring pipeline's trend lines are more defensible than any competitor's. [**Claim**] Because we run deterministic signal extraction on conciseness, thinking-on-the-spot, and handling-pressure, three of our six dimensions are mathematically stable — same input returns the same score today, next week, and after every model release. Yoodli, Speeko, and Orai all run pure-LLM scoring, which means their numbers drift with every model update. We tested this by re-scoring the same audio clip across three weeks and found our composite moved by less than 0.5 points; theirs moved by 7-12. [**Evidence**] That matters because enterprise L&D buyers need to report measurable improvement to their CFO, and drifting numbers get caught in audit. Our defensibility is the difference between a pilot that converts and a pilot that gets killed in month four. [**Implication**]"

## Why Implication is load-bearing

CEI distinguishes itself from similar frameworks because of the *Implication* node. Most argument frameworks stop at evidence and assume the audience will infer the importance. They won't — they'll be too busy evaluating whether the evidence is strong enough. The Implication is where you close the loop: "here's what this fact *means* to you, specifically."

Without Implication, CEI collapses into a fact statement. With it, CEI becomes an argument.

## Common failure modes

- **Vague Claim** — "I think we should probably consider X" is not a claim; it's a hedge. The claim must be stake-worthy.
- **Weak Evidence** — "from what I've seen" or "I've heard that…" is not evidence; it's memory. Cite specifics.
- **Too much Evidence** — drowning the claim in six data points when one or two land harder
- **Missing Implication** — ending at Evidence and leaving the listener to draw the conclusion
- **Circular Evidence** — "it works because it works" or "our users love it because they use it"
- **Evidence that doesn't match the Claim** — a common failure mode where the data supports a related but different claim

## Antipatterns

- "I kind of think maybe…"
- "It seems like…"
- "A lot of people believe…"
- "Studies show…" (without the study)
- "Obviously…" (if it were obvious, you wouldn't need CEI)
- Presenting Evidence first and building to the Claim — that's a different structure entirely

## CEI vs Minto

Use **Minto** (`minto.md`) when the audience is *ready* to hear your answer and you just need to justify it. Three reasons, on-deck evidence.

Use **CEI** when the audience will *contest* your answer and you need each claim to be individually defended. CEI is Minto hardened for debate — single claim, heavy evidence, explicit implication.

In a long-form argument, use both: Minto for the overall structure, CEI for each individual sub-claim that might be attacked.

## Origin

Classical rhetoric, specifically Aristotle's concept of *logos* — argument from logic and evidence. Formalized into modern debate curriculum in the 20th century (cross-examination debate, policy debate). Adapted for business communication in the 1990s as part of structured-argumentation training at firms like McKinsey, BCG, and Bain.
